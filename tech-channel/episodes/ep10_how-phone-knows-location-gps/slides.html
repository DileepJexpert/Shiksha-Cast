Idea 1 / 4 — Clocks in space, shouting

Satellites just broadcast, all the time.

🛰️ Satellite ))) signal ))) 🌍 Everyone

About 30 satellites orbit Earth, shouting to the whole planet.

  • Each one constantly broadcasts two things.
  • Its own position in space.
  • The exact time from an ultra-precise atomic clock.
Katixo KhojLab
Idea 2 / 4 — Turning time into distance

How long it took → how far it is.

  • Your phone catches signals from several satellites.
  • Each signal travels at the speed of light.
  • Measure exactly how long it took to arrive…
  • …and the phone knows how far that satellite is.
The trick

time delay
→ distance

Idea 3 / 4 — Where all the distances meet

One spot fits all of them.

  • One distance puts you somewhere on a giant sphere.
  • Add a second, then a third…
  • …and only ONE point on Earth fits all the distances at once. That's you.
Like three friends

Each says "you're 5 km from me." Only one spot satisfies all three.

This is called trilateration.

Idea 4 / 4 — Your phone never speaks

It only listens. Never transmits.

🛰️ Satellites ))) one way ))) 📱 Phone

Your phone receives only — it never sends anything up.

  • It needs about 4 satellites.
  • 3 to pin the position…
  • …1 more to correct the phone's own clock.
  • More satellites = more accuracy.

Why it matters

This blue dot runs your day.

  • Maps, Uber / Ola, food delivery, fitness trackers — all ride on GPS.
  • Indoors, in tunnels, or between tall buildings? Signals get blocked — so GPS gets weak.
  • Constantly listening to faint space signals is also why GPS can drain your battery.

Common myth — "yeh galat samajhte hain"
✗ Myth

"Satellites track and watch me — my phone talks to the satellites."

✓ Reality

Satellites just broadcast their time and position to the whole world. Your phone silently listens and calculates its own location. The satellites don't even know you exist.

Recap — 2 things to remember
  1. GPS satellites just broadcast their time and position to everyone.
  2. Your phone times those signals to get distances, then trilaterates to pin your exact spot.
