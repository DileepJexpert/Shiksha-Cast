EP07 — What is "the cloud" actually?

ENGLISH
01Title

What is "the cloud" actually? You hear it all the time — "it's in the cloud", "save it to the cloud". But here's a spoiler: it is not in the sky. Let's find out where it really is.

[CUE] Hold on the title ~2s, calm curious tone, then go.
02The Hook

"Don't worry, it's saved in the cloud." We say this almost every day. But the cloud isn't floating around in the sky like a fluffy white blob. It is a very real, very physical place — and today, we're going to go right inside it.

[CUE] Energy up — point upward on "sky", then a "nope" gesture.
03Today's question

So here's today's question: when something is "in the cloud", where does it actually sit — and whose computer is it really on? Let's break it down.

[CUE] Slow down. Let the question land before moving on.
04Idea 1 — Someone else's computers

Idea number one, and it's the big one: "the cloud" simply means other people's computers. These computers are called servers, and you use them over the internet instead of your own phone or laptop. So when something is "in the cloud", it's really just sitting on a company's server somewhere far away. That's it. There's no actual cloud.

[CUE] Land hard on "other people's computers" — this is the whole episode in one line.
05Idea 2 — Data centres are real buildings

So where do these servers actually live? In huge warehouses called data centres. Picture thousands of computers stacked in racks, with massive cooling systems and backup power, running twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week. These are real buildings — in Mumbai, in Hyderabad, all around the world. Your photo travels from your phone, through the internet, into a data centre, onto one specific server.

[CUE] Reveal the chain phone → internet → data centre → server as you say it.
06Idea 3 — You rent, you don't own

Now, why use someone else's computers at all? Because you rent computing and storage instead of buying and maintaining your own. And renting comes with superpowers: you can reach your stuff from any device, anywhere; it scales up instantly when you suddenly need more space; and someone else handles all the boring hardware and maintenance. It's like booking an OYO room instead of buying a whole flat — you just use it and walk away.

[CUE] Reveal the three benefit chips as you list them.
07Idea 4 — Everyday cloud

And here's the thing — you already live in the cloud. Your Google Photos, your WhatsApp chat backup, your Netflix shows, your Gmail — all of these live as files on these servers, and they stream down to you on demand whenever you open the app. So really, "the cloud" just means renting space on the internet and reaching it from anywhere.

[CUE] Reveal the app chips one by one — make it feel familiar.
08Why it matters

Once you get this, a lot of everyday stuff suddenly makes sense. "Storage full — buy more cloud"? That's just renting more space on those servers. No internet means you can't reach the cloud, because your stuff is sitting far away. Your backups survive even if your phone breaks, because the copy lives in a data centre. And one thing to remember: your files are sitting on someone else's computer — so privacy genuinely matters here.

[CUE] Slightly serious on the privacy line — it's important.
09Common myth

Which brings us to the biggest myth. People imagine the cloud is wireless, floating in the air — some kind of magical, infinite storage. It's not. It's physical servers in big buildings, owned by companies, reached over the internet. The cloud is very much on the ground.

[CUE] Reveal the green "Reality" card after a beat on the myth.
10Recap

Quick recap. One: the cloud is just other people's servers in data centres, used over the internet. Two: you rent it — they own it and maintain it for you. Simple as that.

[CUE] Crisp and clear — these two lines are the takeaway.
