Why do computers only understand 0s and 1s? Every photo, every song, every video on your phone — secretly, it's all just two digits. Today, let's find out why.

02The Hook

Look at your phone. Every photo, every song, every video, every WhatsApp message — all of it, deep down, is just zeros and ones. And here's the weird part: we humans count with ten digits, zero to nine. So why does the most powerful machine ever built use only two? Stick around — in five minutes this whole mystery disappears.

[CUE] Energy up — this is the hook. Hold up the phone on "every photo, every song".
03Today's question

So here's today's one question: why does a computer use just two symbols — and how on earth can two symbols store everything? Let's find out.

[CUE] Slow down. Let the question land before moving on.
04Idea 1 — Switches

Idea number one: a computer is built from billions of microscopic switches. Each switch is called a transistor. And a switch can only be in two positions — off, or on. That's it. We just give those two positions names: off we call zero, on we call one. That's the computer's entire alphabet — two letters.

[CUE] Mime flicking a light switch on "off… on". Reveal 0 then 1.
05Idea 2 — Two = reliable

Now the big question — why two? Why not ten, like us? Here's the key insight. Two states are incredibly easy to tell apart — just like a light switch is clearly on or clearly off, which inside the chip means a high voltage or a low voltage. But imagine ten different voltage levels squeezed in. Was that a six or a seven? Tiny bit of noise and you'd make a mistake. So two states means simple, and simple means reliable, and reliable means fast. That's the whole reason.

[CUE] Land hard on "reliable" — this is the most important idea of the video.
06Idea 3 — Combine bits

"But two options is so little!" — right? One switch on its own can only say zero or one. The trick is, you line them up. Each zero or one is called a bit. Put four bits in a row — like one-zero-one-zero — and reading them by place value, that's eight plus two, which equals ten. Stack eight bits together and you get a byte — that's two hundred and fifty-six different combinations. Add enough bits, and you can represent any number at all.

[CUE] Reveal the 8-4-2-1 columns, then the "= 10" result. Read it slowly.
07Idea 4 — Everything becomes numbers

Okay — but a photo isn't a number, a song isn't a number. So here's the final move. Everything first gets turned into numbers. Letters get a number code — that's ASCII or Unicode. Colours become numbers — a red, green and blue value for each dot. Sound becomes numbers — thousands of tiny samples per second. And once it's all numbers, every number becomes binary. So one beautifully simple system stores absolutely everything.

[CUE] Reveal letters → colours → sound rows one at a time as you list them.
08Why it matters

And this is the beautiful payoff. This is exactly why "digital" is so reliable and so cheap. Two clean states — on or off — is why your computer almost never makes a mistake reading data. And because each switch is so simple, you can shrink it down and pack billions of them onto a single chip — tiny, cheap, and fast. The entire digital world stands on that one idea.

[CUE] Confident, slightly awed tone — this is the "wow, that's elegant" beat.
09Common myth

Which clears up a big myth. People think binary is some weird, mysterious secret code that only computers can understand. It really isn't. It's just counting with two symbols instead of ten — and the only reason it's two is that the hardware physically has two states: on-off switches. There's nothing magical about it at all.

[CUE] Reveal the green "Reality" card after a short beat on the myth.
10Recap

Quick recap. One: a computer is just billions of on-off switches — so its whole alphabet is zero and one. Two: two states are reliable, and combining enough of them can represent any data — a number, a letter, a photo, a song.
